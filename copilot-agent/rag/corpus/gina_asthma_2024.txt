[GINA 2024 §1.1] Asthma Diagnosis
Asthma diagnosis requires a history of variable respiratory symptoms (wheeze, shortness of breath, chest tightness, cough) AND evidence of variable expiratory airflow limitation. Confirmatory tests: spirometry showing FEV1/FVC ratio reduced and demonstrating reversibility (≥12% AND ≥200 mL increase in FEV1 after bronchodilator), peak expiratory flow (PEF) variability >10% in adults, OR positive bronchial provocation test. Symptoms typically vary over time and in intensity, are often triggered by exercise, allergens, weather changes, or viral infections, and frequently worsen at night or early morning.
---
[GINA 2024 §2.1] Track 1 (Preferred): ICS-Formoterol as Both Reliever and Controller
GINA 2024 strongly recommends Track 1 over the older Track 2 for most adults and adolescents ≥12. Track 1 uses low-dose ICS-formoterol (e.g., budesonide-formoterol, beclomethasone-formoterol) as the reliever instead of SABA. This is called Anti-Inflammatory Reliever (AIR) therapy. Reasons: SABA-only treatment is associated with increased exacerbation risk and asthma deaths; ICS-formoterol used as needed delivers anti-inflammatory dose at the moment of symptoms, reducing exacerbations by ~64% versus SABA alone (SYGMA trials).
---
[GINA 2024 §2.2] Track 1 Stepwise Approach
Step 1 (mild symptoms <2x/month): low-dose ICS-formoterol as needed. Step 2 (symptoms ≥2x/month but not daily): low-dose ICS-formoterol as needed (same as step 1) — this is the preferred regimen. Step 3 (symptoms most days, or waking with asthma ≥1x/week): low-dose maintenance ICS-formoterol PLUS as-needed ICS-formoterol (MART regimen — Maintenance and Reliever Therapy). Step 4 (daily symptoms or waking ≥1x/week with low FEV1): medium-dose maintenance ICS-formoterol with as-needed ICS-formoterol. Step 5 (severe asthma): refer to specialist; add LAMA (tiotropium) or biologic (omalizumab, mepolizumab, dupilumab) based on phenotype.
---
[GINA 2024 §2.3] Track 2 (Alternative): Maintenance ICS plus SABA Reliever
Track 2 is reserved for patients unwilling or unable to use ICS-formoterol or where ICS-formoterol is unavailable. Step 1: as-needed SABA plus low-dose ICS taken whenever SABA is taken. Step 2: daily low-dose ICS plus as-needed SABA. Step 3: low-dose ICS-LABA plus as-needed SABA. Step 4: medium-dose ICS-LABA plus as-needed SABA. Step 5: high-dose ICS-LABA, refer to specialist, add LAMA or biologic. Patients on Track 2 should be reviewed regularly and offered transition to Track 1 if appropriate.
---
[GINA 2024 §3.1] Reliever Overuse Is a Red Flag
SABA use of ≥3 canisters per year (12 puffs/canister, ~36/year) is associated with increased exacerbation risk; ≥12 canisters/year is associated with increased asthma mortality. Excessive reliever use signals poor asthma control and inadequate anti-inflammatory therapy. Action: review inhaler technique, adherence to controller, exposure to triggers, and step up controller therapy. The presence of reliever overuse alone is sufficient indication to step up — do not wait for spirometry deterioration.
---
[GINA 2024 §4.1] Asthma Control Assessment
At every visit, assess: (1) symptom control over past 4 weeks: daytime symptoms >2x/week, any night-time waking, reliever needed for symptoms >2x/week, any activity limitation. None = well-controlled, 1–2 = partly-controlled, 3–4 = uncontrolled. (2) future risk: history of exacerbations in past year, low FEV1, high SABA use, smoking, comorbidities (rhinitis, GERD, obesity, food allergy), eosinophilia. (3) treatment issues: inhaler technique, adherence, side effects, comorbidities. Validated tools: Asthma Control Test (ACT) — score ≥20 well-controlled, 16–19 not well-controlled, ≤15 poorly controlled.
---
[GINA 2024 §4.2] When to Step Up Therapy
Consider stepping up if asthma is partly-controlled or uncontrolled, OR if exacerbation has occurred. Before stepping up: confirm diagnosis, check inhaler technique (most common cause of "treatment failure"), check adherence, address modifiable risk factors (smoking, allergens, comorbidities). Sustained step-up: if symptoms persist 2–3 months after addressing modifiable factors. Short-term step-up: 1–2 weeks during viral URI or seasonal allergen exposure. Day-to-day adjustment via personalized action plan: if symptoms worsen, increase reliever frequency and/or temporarily increase ICS dose.
---
[GINA 2024 §4.3] When to Step Down Therapy
Consider step-down once asthma has been well-controlled for 3 months on current therapy, with no exacerbations in past year. Reduce step-by-step, monitoring symptoms and lung function. Avoid step-down during pregnancy, viral season, or upcoming travel. Do NOT stop ICS entirely as monotherapy step-down — minimum is low-dose ICS or low-dose ICS-formoterol as needed. Document a written asthma action plan after each step-down. Re-evaluate at 3 months.
---
[GINA 2024 §5.1] Acute Exacerbation Management — Mild to Moderate
Mild-to-moderate exacerbation features: increased symptoms, PEF 50–75% personal best or predicted, no signs of severe attack. Initial treatment: 4–10 puffs of SABA via spacer (or 4 puffs ICS-formoterol if MART regimen), repeat every 20 minutes for first hour. Add oral prednisolone 1 mg/kg up to 50 mg/day for 5–7 days for adults if response is incomplete or patient is on regular ICS. No tapering needed if duration ≤7 days. Reassess in 1 hour. Discharge home with action plan, follow-up within 1 week.
---
[GINA 2024 §5.2] Acute Exacerbation Management — Severe and Life-Threatening
Severe attack: cannot complete sentences, RR >30, HR >120, SpO2 <90%, PEF <50% personal best. Life-threatening: silent chest, exhaustion, cyanosis, altered mental status, bradycardia, hypotension. Treatment: high-dose oxygen to SpO2 93–95%, continuous nebulized SABA, ipratropium 0.5 mg via nebulizer Q20 min for first hour, IV magnesium sulfate 2 g over 20 min for life-threatening or refractory cases, oral or IV corticosteroid (methylprednisolone 40–80 mg). Admit if poor response in 1 hour. Intubation for impending respiratory failure.
---
[GINA 2024 §6.1] Comorbidities Affecting Asthma Control
Allergic rhinitis: treat with intranasal corticosteroid; improves asthma control. Chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps: associated with severe asthma; consider biologic therapy. GERD: treat if symptomatic — does not improve asthma control if asymptomatic. Obesity: 5–10% weight loss improves control; bariatric surgery for severe asthma + BMI ≥35 may be beneficial. OSA: treat with CPAP. Smoking: most important modifiable factor; smoking reduces ICS efficacy. Anxiety/depression: common in severe asthma, addresses through medical and psychological care.
---
[GINA 2024 §6.2] Inhaler Technique and Adherence
Up to 70–80% of patients use inhalers incorrectly. Check technique at every visit; demonstrate the device every visit; ask about adherence non-judgmentally. Most common errors: not exhaling before inhalation, not holding breath after inhalation, poor coordination with pMDI (use spacer), slow inhalation when fast required (DPI). Adherence often <50%; ask "how often do you forget to take your medication in a typical week?" Pharmacy refill data is the most objective measure. Address adherence barriers: side effects, complexity, cost, beliefs, depression.
