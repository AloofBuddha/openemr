[USPSTF 2023 §1.1] Depression Screening — Adults
The USPSTF recommends screening for depression in all adults, including pregnant and postpartum persons (Grade B). Screening should be implemented with adequate systems in place to ensure accurate diagnosis, effective treatment, and appropriate follow-up. The optimal screening interval is not established; pragmatic strategies include screening all new patients, then periodically thereafter (e.g., annually), with consideration of risk factors. Risk factors include personal or family history, chronic illness, recent loss, substance use, and other psychosocial stressors.
---
[USPSTF 2023 §1.2] PHQ-9 Tool and Scoring
The Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) is a 9-item self-administered tool covering DSM-5 depression criteria over the past 2 weeks. Each item scored 0–3 (not at all, several days, more than half the days, nearly every day). Total score 0–27. Severity bands: 0–4 minimal, 5–9 mild, 10–14 moderate, 15–19 moderately severe, 20–27 severe. Score ≥10 has 88% sensitivity and 88% specificity for major depressive disorder. PHQ-2 (first 2 questions) is a brief pre-screen; if PHQ-2 ≥3, administer the full PHQ-9.
---
[USPSTF 2023 §1.3] PHQ-9 Item 9 — Suicide Risk
PHQ-9 item 9 ("Thoughts that you would be better off dead, or of hurting yourself") flags acute suicide risk. Any positive response (score ≥1) requires same-day assessment. Use a structured tool such as the Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) or PHQ-9 follow-up questions: passive ideation (wishing to be dead), active ideation (thoughts of self-harm), plan, intent, prior attempts, access to means. Patients with active ideation, a plan, or recent attempt require immediate psychiatric evaluation. Document risk assessment and management plan in chart.
---
[USPSTF 2023 §2.1] Depression Treatment — First-Line Options
For mild depression: psychotherapy (CBT, IPT, behavioral activation) is first line; pharmacotherapy may be added if no improvement at 4–8 weeks. For moderate-severe depression: pharmacotherapy plus psychotherapy is most effective. SSRIs are first-line: sertraline 25–200 mg, escitalopram 10–20 mg, fluoxetine 20–80 mg, citalopram 20–40 mg. SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine) are alternatives, particularly when neuropathic pain coexists. Bupropion is useful for patients who want to avoid sexual side effects or sedation. Mirtazapine for patients with insomnia or weight loss.
---
[USPSTF 2023 §2.2] Antidepressant Initiation and Monitoring
Start at low dose, titrate to therapeutic dose by week 4. Onset of effect: 2–4 weeks for energy/sleep, 4–6 weeks for mood. Follow up at 2 weeks (assess tolerance, side effects, suicide risk — slight increase in young adults), 6 weeks (assess response — at least 50% PHQ-9 reduction), 12 weeks (remission goal — PHQ-9 <5). If no response by 6 weeks: assess adherence, reassess diagnosis, optimize dose. If partial response: increase dose, augment with bupropion, mirtazapine, or atypical antipsychotic. If no response: switch to different SSRI or SNRI.
---
[USPSTF 2023 §2.3] Antidepressant Discontinuation
Continue antidepressant for at least 6 months after remission for first episode, 12 months for recurrent depression, indefinitely for chronic or recurrent (≥3 episodes) depression. Taper gradually over 4 weeks to reduce discontinuation symptoms (dizziness, GI symptoms, flu-like symptoms, mood lability) — particularly for paroxetine and venlafaxine due to short half-lives. Fluoxetine has long half-life and may not require taper. Counsel patients to never stop abruptly. Address recurrence risk and warning signs at each visit.

[USPSTF 2024 §3.1] Unhealthy Alcohol Use Screening — Adults
The USPSTF recommends screening adults age 18+ for unhealthy alcohol use in primary care, and providing brief behavioral counseling intervention to reduce unhealthy alcohol use (Grade B). Unhealthy use spans a continuum from risky drinking through alcohol use disorder. Recommended limits: men ≤2 drinks/day and ≤14/week, women and adults >65 ≤1 drink/day and ≤7/week. One standard drink = 14 g pure alcohol = 12 oz beer = 5 oz wine = 1.5 oz spirits.
---
[USPSTF 2024 §3.2] AUDIT-C Tool and Scoring
AUDIT-C (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test - Consumption) is a 3-item screen: (1) frequency of drinking days, (2) typical drinks per drinking day, (3) frequency of ≥6 drinks in one occasion. Each item scored 0–4; total 0–12. Cutoffs: men ≥4, women ≥3 indicate unhealthy use. Higher scores correlate with severity of alcohol use disorder. Always follow positive screen with full AUDIT (10 items) or DSM-5 alcohol use disorder criteria. Document the score and any planned intervention.
---
[USPSTF 2024 §3.3] Brief Intervention for Alcohol Use
For patients screening positive on AUDIT-C: deliver a 5–15 minute brief intervention using FRAMES principles — Feedback (review screening result), Responsibility (patient's choice to change), Advice (clear recommendation to reduce), Menu of options, Empathy, Self-efficacy. Set a goal (e.g., abstain or reduce to within recommended limits). Provide written self-help materials. Schedule follow-up in 4–6 weeks. Brief interventions reduce alcohol use by 4–5 drinks/week and 12% reduction in heavy drinking episodes — comparable effect size to smoking cessation counseling.
---
[USPSTF 2024 §3.4] Alcohol Use Disorder — Pharmacotherapy
For moderate-to-severe alcohol use disorder, FDA-approved pharmacotherapy is underused (only ~9% of eligible patients). First-line options: naltrexone 50 mg/day oral OR 380 mg IM monthly (mu-opioid receptor antagonist, reduces craving and reward). Acamprosate 666 mg three times daily (modulates glutamate, useful for maintenance abstinence). Disulfiram 250 mg daily (causes aversive reaction with alcohol; requires high motivation). Combine with psychosocial therapy (motivational interviewing, CBT, mutual-help groups). Treat co-occurring depression or anxiety.
---
[USPSTF 2024 §4.1] SSRI-Alcohol Interaction Counseling
SSRIs combined with alcohol: increased sedation, impaired judgment, possible escalation of mood symptoms in the day after binge drinking, and small increased GI bleeding risk on chronic SSRI plus heavy alcohol use. Patients with depression AND unhealthy alcohol use require both conditions addressed — alcohol can mask, mimic, or worsen depression and reduce SSRI efficacy. Sertraline and escitalopram are safest in patients who continue to drink moderately. Bupropion is contraindicated in patients with seizure history including alcohol withdrawal seizures.
---
[USPSTF 2024 §4.2] Co-Occurring Depression and Alcohol Use
Patients with both major depressive disorder and alcohol use disorder have worse outcomes for both conditions. Treat both simultaneously — do not require alcohol abstinence before initiating depression treatment. Antidepressants alone are insufficient if alcohol use is heavy; specific AUD pharmacotherapy plus psychotherapy improves outcomes. Reassess depression severity after 2–4 weeks of reduced alcohol intake — depression often improves with reduced drinking even before antidepressant takes effect. Refer to dual-diagnosis program when available.
